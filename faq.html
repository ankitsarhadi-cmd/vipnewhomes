Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before buying a pre-construction home in Ontario.

Getting Started

Before a new development opens to the public, builders offer a short window of early access to registered agents and their clients. During VIP Access, you get first pick of lots, models, and floor plans — often at pre-launch pricing before any increases. The best corner lots, largest models, and premium elevations sell out before most buyers even know the project exists. VIP registration through us is free and costs you nothing.
With resale, you buy an existing home and move in within 30–90 days. With pre-construction, you're buying a home that hasn't been built yet — you sign today and take possession when construction is complete (typically 12–36 months later). The key advantages of pre-con:
  • You lock in today's price while values may rise during construction
  • Deposits are spread over time, not due all at once
  • Brand-new home — no renovations, no history
  • Full Tarion warranty coverage
  • Ability to choose finishes, upgrades, and sometimes layouts

The Purchase Process

Deposits vary by builder but typically total 15–20% of the purchase price, paid in stages over 12–24 months. A common structure:
  • 5% on signing
  • 5% at 90 days
  • 5% at 180 days
  • Balance to 20% at 365 days (if required)
This staged structure gives you time to arrange financing without a large upfront lump sum. We review the exact deposit schedule with you before you sign.
The APS is the legal contract between you and the builder. It outlines the purchase price, deposit schedule, closing date, permitted delays, included features, and your rights under Tarion. We attend every signing with you, and we connect you with a real estate lawyer to review the contract before you're bound to anything. Never sign a builder APS without legal review.
Ontario law gives you a 10-calendar-day cooling-off period after signing a new-home APS. During this time, you can cancel the agreement for any reason and receive a full refund of your deposit — no penalty. Use this window to have a lawyer review the contract thoroughly. We always ensure this review happens before the window closes.
Yes — most builders schedule a decor/design appointment during construction where you select finishes, colours, flooring, and upgrade options. The scope and timing depends on the builder and construction stage. We advise on which upgrades offer the best value at resale and which to skip.

Costs & Financials

Yes — and under Ontario's updated 2026 rules (for purchase agreements signed April 1, 2026 – March 31, 2027), both primary residences and investment/rental properties qualify for the HST rebate. The rebate is up to $130,000 maximum: on homes priced at $1M or under, the full 13% HST is rebated; between $1M–$1.5M the rebate is capped at $130K; and it phases out above $1.5M. For primary residences, most builders already factor the rebate into the advertised price — so what you see on the price sheet is typically what you pay. Use our HST Rebate Calculator for a precise estimate, and confirm your specific situation with your lawyer.
Development charges (DCs) are fees municipalities impose on builders to fund infrastructure — roads, sewers, schools. Many builders cap their DCs, meaning any increases after a set date are absorbed by the builder, not passed to you. Others have no cap, meaning charges can increase between signing and closing. We highlight this in every project review — it can be a significant difference between projects.
In addition to your purchase price, budget for:
  • Land Transfer Tax — provincial (and municipal in Toronto). First-time buyers receive a rebate
  • Legal fees — typically $1,500–$3,000
  • Tarion enrolment fee — paid to the builder, already included in most prices
  • Utility connections, meter installations — varies by builder
  • Adjustments — property tax, utility, and condo fee adjustments at closing
We walk you through a full closing cost estimate before you sign.

During Construction

Typically 12 to 36 months, depending on the project stage when you buy. Some communities are already under construction (shorter timeline), while others are in early planning (longer). The APS will specify an estimated closing date range. We'll clearly outline the expected timeline for any project before you commit.
Delays are common in construction. Ontario's Tarion regulations allow builders a set number of permitted delays (outlined in your APS) with proper written notice. If the builder delays beyond the permitted extensions, you have the right to cancel and receive your deposit back. We monitor any delay notices alongside your lawyer and advise you on your options.
An assignment means selling your purchase contract to another buyer before you take possession. Many builders allow this, but policies and fees vary — some charge an assignment fee of $5,000–$10,000 or require builder consent. Assignment clauses must be reviewed before signing the APS. We flag assignment terms as part of every deal review.

Warranty & Protection

Tarion is Ontario's government-backed new home warranty program. Every new home built by a licensed builder in Ontario is covered:
  • 1 year — defects in work and materials
  • 2 years — electrical, plumbing, heating, and building envelope
  • 7 years — major structural defects
This protection is one of the strongest arguments for buying new over resale.
Yes — Tarion's deposit protection covers up to $100,000 for freehold homes and condos in Ontario. This means if your builder becomes insolvent before closing, your deposit is protected up to that limit. We only work with established, reputable builders to minimize this risk from the outset.
Before you take possession, the builder invites you to a Pre-Delivery Inspection — a walkthrough of your completed home to document any deficiencies. This is your opportunity to note items that need to be repaired before or shortly after you move in. We recommend bringing a checklist and, ideally, a third-party inspector. Everything you document at the PDI is the builder's responsibility to fix under Tarion's first-year warranty.

Financing & Strategy

No mortgage is required at signing — you only need your deposit funds. The full mortgage is arranged closer to your closing date, which could be 12–36 months away. This is actually an advantage: you have time to save, pay down debt, and strengthen your financial position before the mortgage is needed. We connect every buyer with a mortgage broker who specializes in pre-construction and can hold a rate guarantee well ahead of closing.
This is a real concern and one we take seriously upfront. We never encourage a buyer to sign an APS without confidence in their ability to close. Before signing, we ensure you've spoken with a qualified mortgage broker and have a realistic financing plan. If circumstances change — job loss, rule changes, stress test tightening — there are options including assignment sales (selling your contract to another buyer) or negotiating with the builder. The worst outcome is always avoidable with early planning.
Yes — the First Home Savings Account (FHSA) and RRSP Home Buyers' Plan both apply to pre-construction. The FHSA lets first-time buyers contribute up to $8,000/year (lifetime max $40,000) tax-free, with withdrawals tax-free for a qualifying home purchase. The RRSP Home Buyers' Plan allows a withdrawal of up to $35,000 per person ($70,000 per couple). Since closing is months or years away, you have time to maximize these accounts before you need the funds. We walk every first-time buyer through this in our initial consultation.
Permanent residents can buy on the same terms as Canadian citizens. Some exceptions apply — including certain rural areas and properties with 4+ units. If you're on a work permit or in the process of gaining PR, your eligibility depends on your specific status. We'll connect you with a real estate lawyer to confirm your eligibility before you register for any project.

This is one of the most misunderstood distinctions in Ontario pre-construction:
  • Freehold townhouse — you own the land and structure outright. No monthly maintenance fees. You're responsible for all maintenance. This is the preferred type for most buyers.
  • Condo townhouse — looks identical from the outside, but the land is owned by the condo corporation. You pay monthly maintenance fees (typically $200–$500/month) covering common areas, building insurance, and sometimes utilities. You have less control over the property.
The distinction significantly affects long-term cost and resale value. We flag this on every project we present.
